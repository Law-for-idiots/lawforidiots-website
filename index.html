Labour Law - laws that protects workers

Labour law consists of any law that governs the employee/employer relationship. There are a number of labour laws but the most important to know are the:

    Occupational and Health Safety Act of 1993

    The Occupational Health and Safety Act of 1993 is a South African law that provides for the health and safety of people at work or in connection with the use of plant and machinery. It further provides for the protection of people other than people at work from hazards arising out of or in connection with the activities of people at work.

    The Act places the primary responsibility for health and safety on the employer. The employer must take all reasonable steps to ensure the health and safety of their employees and other people who may be affected by their activities. This includes:

    • Identifying and assessing the hazards in the workplace
    • Taking steps to control the hazards
    • Providing information and training to employees on health and safety
    • Ensuring that the workplace is safe and healthy

    The Act also gives employees certain rights, including the right to:

    • Be informed about health and safety risks in the workplace
    • Participate in health and safety decision-making
    • Refuse to work in unsafe conditions

    The Act is enforced by the Department of Employment and Labour. If an employer breaches the Act, they may be prosecuted and fined or imprisoned.

    Here are some of the key provisions of the Occupational Health and Safety Act:

    • The Act applies to all workplaces, regardless of the size or industry.
    • The employer is responsible for the health and safety of all employees and other people who may be affected by their activities.
    • Employees have the right to be informed about health and safety risks in the workplace and to participate in health and safety decision-making.
    • The Act sets out a number of specific requirements for employers, such as the need to identify and assess hazards, take steps to control hazards, and provide information and training to employees
    • The Act is enforced by the Department of Employment and Labour

    The Occupational Health and Safety Act is an important piece of legislation that helps to protect the health and safety of workers in South Africa. It is essential for employers to understand their obligations under the Act and to take steps to comply with it.

  • Labour Relations Act of 1995

    The Labour Relations Act of 1995 is a comprehensive piece of legislation that regulates labour relations in South Africa. It was enacted in 1995 and is enforced by the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA).

    The LRA seeks to balance the rights of employees and employers, and to promote industrial harmony. It does this by:

    • Giving employees the right to form and join trade unions, and to bargain collectively with their employers
    • Regulating the right to strike and lockout
    • Establishing a framework for resolving labour disputes through conciliation, mediation, and arbitration
    • Promoting employee participation in decision-making in the workplace

    The LRA also sets out a number of fundamental principles that must be followed in all labour relations, including:

    • The right to fair labour practices.
    • The right to freedom of association
    • The right to collective bargaining
    • The right to strike and lockout
    • The right to participate in decision-making in the workplace

    The LRA is a complex piece of legislation, and employers and employees should seek legal advice if they have any questions about their rights and responsibilities under the Act

    Here are some of the key provisions of the LRA

    • Trade union rights: The LRA guarantees the right of employees to form and join trade unions, and to participate in the activities of trade unions
    • Collective bargaining: The LRA establishes a framework for collective bargaining between employers and trade unions. This includes the right to negotiate wages, working conditions, and other terms and conditions of employment
    • Strikes and lockouts: The LRA regulates the right to strike and lockout. Strikes and lockouts are only allowed in certain circumstances, and they must be conducted in a fair and orderly manner
    • Dispute resolution: The LRA establishes a number of mechanisms for resolving labour disputes, including conciliation, mediation, and arbitration
    • Employee participation: The LRA promotes employee participation in decision-making in the workplace. This can be done through workplace forums, joint consultation committees, or other mechanisms

    The LRA is an important piece of legislation that helps to protect the rights of employees and employers in South Africa. It is a complex Act, and employers and employees should seek legal advice if they have any questions about their rights and responsibilities under the Act

  • Basic Conditions of Employment Act of 1997

    The Basic Conditions of Employment Act of 1997 is a comprehensive piece of legislation that sets out the minimum standards for basic conditions of employment in South Africa. It was enacted in 1997 and is enforced by the Department of Employment and Labour

    The BCEA covers a wide range of topics, including:

    • Hours of work: The BCEA limits the number of hours that an employee can work per day and per week. It also requires employers to provide employees with a meal interval and rest period
    • Overtime: The BCEA allows employers to require employees to work overtime, but only with the employee's consent. Overtime must be paid at a higher rate than normal wages
    • Paid leave: The BCEA entitles employees to paid annual leave, sick leave, maternity leave, and family responsibility leave
    • Remuneration: The BCEA sets a minimum wage for employees. It also requires employers to pay employees their wages on time and in full
    • Discrimination: The BCEA prohibits discrimination in employment on the basis of race, gender, religion, sexual orientation, or other grounds
    • Health and safety: The BCEA requires employers to provide a safe and healthy working environment for their employees

    The BCEA is an important piece of legislation that helps to protect the rights of employees in South Africa. It is a complex Act, and employers and employees should seek legal advice if they have any questions about their rights and responsibilities under the Act

  • Employment Equity Act of 1998

    The Employment Equity Act of 1998 is a South African law that promotes equity in the workplace by eliminating unfair discrimination and implementing affirmative action measures. The EEA applies to all employers, regardless of size, in the private and public sectors

    The EEA prohibits unfair discrimination in all employment practices, including recruitment, selection, training, promotion, remuneration, and termination of employment. It also prohibits discrimination in the provision of benefits, facilities, and services.

    The EEA defines unfair discrimination as any act or omission that directly or indirectly disadvantages an employee or applicant for employment on one or more of the following grounds:

    • Race
    • Gender
    • Sex
    • Pregnancy
    • Marital status
    • Family responsibility
    • Ethnic or social origin
    • Colour
    • Sexual orientation
    • Age
    • Disability
    • Religion
    • HIV status
    • Conscience, belief, political opinion, culture, language, and birth

    The EEA does not prohibit all discrimination. It is not unfair discrimination to promote affirmative action measures that are consistent with the Act. Affirmative action measures are designed to redress the disadvantages in employment experienced by designated groups, which are black people, women, and people with disabilities

    Employers are required to take steps to eliminate unfair discrimination in their workplaces. These steps include:

    • Developing and implementing an employment equity plan
    • Conducting a skills audit
    • Setting numerical goals for the representation of designated groups in their workforce
    • Taking measures to achieve these goals

    Employers who fail to comply with the EEA may be subject to a number of sanctions, including fines, orders to comply, and compensation for employees who have been discriminated against

    The EEA is an important piece of legislation that seeks to promote equality in the South African workplace. It is a complex law, and employers should seek legal advice to ensure that they are complying with its requirements.

Any infringement in regard to the above labour laws can be dealt with via the relevant governing body such as the:

  • Department of Labour (DoL)

    The Department of Labour deals with managing general issues between the employee/employer.

    Here are some of the issues that the DoL deals with:

    1. Unlawful withholding of salary
    2. Unlawful salary deductions
    3. Labour site inspections
    4. Unemployment insurance fund applications

    If you have any issue with your employer, you can raise it with any of the Chief Labour Inspectors at the DoL. The inspector will assess your matter and determine whether it is of the jurisdiction of the DoL or CCMA

    If the DoL is unable to resolve your matter, it is best to refer your matter to the CCMA

  • CCMA

    The Council for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) deals with issues that the DoL is unable to fulfill.

    Here are some of the issues the CCMA deals with:

    1. Unfair dismissal
    2. Unfair labour practices
    3. Discrimination
    4. Wage disputes
    5. Other labour-related disputes

    Think of the CCMA as a step above the DoL in terms of the level of issues that are dealt with, if you have an issue that you would like resolved between you and your employer, you can lodge it through their online portal

  • Labour Court

    The Labour Court is at the same level of the High Court, you typically get here if your matter is not able to be resolved through the DoL or CCMA. In most cases, you come to the Labour Court if you were unhappy with the judgement made at the CCMA or if your matter is of a serious nature.

This act will protect you from being demoted, fired or harassed by your employer. This is called the act of whistleblowing.

Tort Law - laws that protect people from harm caused by others

Tort law is a branch of civil law that deals with wrongful acts that cause harm to another person or property. Torts can be intentional, such as assault and battery, or unintentional, such as negligence. The purpose of tort law is to provide compensation to the injured party and to deter others from committing similar acts

  • Protection Orders

    A protection order is a court order that prohibits a person from contacting or harming another person. It is also known as a restraining order or a domestic violence interdict. Protection orders can be issued in a variety of situations, including domestic violence, stalking, harassment, and sexual assault

    To get a protection order, the protected person must file a petition with the court. The petition must allege that the respondent has engaged in conduct that warrants the issuance of a protection order. The court will then hold a hearing to decide whether to issue the order.

Administrative Law - laws that deal with the relationship between government and citizens

Administrative law is the set of rules that tells government agencies how to do their jobs. It's like a rulebook for government workers. Administrative law is important because it helps to make sure that government agencies are fair and accountable to the people. It also helps to make sure that government agencies follow the law.

  • Lodging a complaint against government and government officials

    There are a few different ways to lodge a complaint against a government or a government official in South Africa. The best way to lodge a complaint will depend on the specific circumstances of your complaint.

    Here are some of the options available to you:

    • Lodge a complaint with the Public Protector: The Public Protector is an independent institution that is tasked with investigating and remedying cases of maladministration, abuse of power, and corruption in the public sector. You can lodge a complaint with the Public Protector online, by mail, or in person.
    • Lodge a complaint with the relevant government department: If your complaint is about a specific government department, you can lodge a complaint with that department directly. The department will have its own complaints procedure, which you can find on their website or by contacting the department.
    • Lodge a complaint with the South African Human Rights Commission: The South African Human Rights Commission is an independent institution that is tasked with promoting and protecting human rights in South Africa. You can lodge a complaint with the Human Rights Commission if you believe that your rights have been violated by a government official.

    If you are not sure which body to lodge your complaint with, you can contact the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development for assistance.
